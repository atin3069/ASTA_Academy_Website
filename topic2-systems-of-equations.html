The key idea

A system of equations has two equations and two unknowns. Your job is to find the values of both variables that satisfy both equations at the same time. The SAT tests this in three distinct ways — and knowing all three is what separates a 650 from a 750.

✕
One solution
Lines intersect at one point. Most common case.
∥
No solution
Lines are parallel. Same slope, different intercepts.
∞
Infinite solutions
Same line. Both equations are identical when simplified.

The highlighted cases — no solution and infinite solutions — are where the SAT loves to trip students up. Master these and you gain points others give away.

Worked examples

Example 1 Elimination method
Solve the system: 2x + 3y = 12  and  4x − 3y = 6
Elimination
Notice: the y-coefficients are opposites (+3 and −3)
  • 1Add the equations together:  (2x + 4x) + (3y − 3y) = 12 + 6
  • 2Simplify:  6x = 18  →  x = 3
  • 3Substitute x = 3 into the first equation:  2(3) + 3y = 12  →  3y = 6  →  y = 2
Answer: x = 3, y = 2
Dr. Jain's tip Use elimination when the coefficients of one variable are already opposites (or easy multiples). It's usually faster than substitution on the SAT.
Example 2 Substitution method
Solve the system: y = 2x − 1  and  3x + y = 14
Substitution
Use substitution when one variable is already isolated
  • 1The first equation already gives us y. Substitute into the second:  3x + (2x − 1) = 14
  • 2Simplify:  5x − 1 = 14  →  5x = 15  →  x = 3
  • 3Find y:  y = 2(3) − 1 = 5
Answer: x = 3, y = 5
Example 3 The no-solution trap
How many solutions does this system have?   2x + 4y = 8  and  x + 2y = 6
Special case
  • 1Divide the first equation by 2:  x + 2y = 4
  • 2Compare with the second equation:  x + 2y = 6
  • 3Same left side, different right side — this is a contradiction. The lines are parallel.
Answer: No solution
Dr. Jain's tip For no-solution questions, the SAT often gives you a system with a missing constant and asks for its value. Always simplify both equations to the same form first, then compare — if left sides match but right sides differ, it's no solution; if both sides match, it's infinite solutions.

Common trap to avoid

⚠  No-solution vs. infinite solutions These two cases look similar but mean opposite things. When you reduce both equations to the same form: if the right-hand sides are different → no solution. If the right-hand sides are identical → infinite solutions. The SAT uses this distinction to eliminate students who memorize steps without understanding the geometry.
